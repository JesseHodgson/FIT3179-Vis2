Where are the World's Volcanoes?

Throughout the planet, whether above land, or under the waves, you can find a volcano if you look for long enough. Sure enough, there's alot; over recorded history, we have data from a little more than 1500 volcanoes from aorund the planet. For an untrained eye, it may look like there is no rhyme or reason to where these volcanoes appear, however, the majority of volcanoes appear on or around a tectonic fault line.

This is becasue at the joints of two tectonic plates, in many cases, one plate sinks below the other one, forcing magma from below the surface of the Earth to burst through the mantle, and violently release their energy by spewing their magma all around.

The above diagram graph shows the locations of the worlds volcanoes, marked with red dots on the map, and each contininent is coloured to show how many volcanoes are in each continent. North America has some of the most volcanoes in the world, largely due to the fact that it sits on the edge of the pacific plate in line with what is commonly called "The Ring of Fire", which is the ring-shaped volcano system along the rim of the Pacific Plate.

Where can we see the most volcanoes?

America is the country with the single most volcanoes in it, with a whopping 205 volcanoes amongst all it's states. The state with the most number volcanoes is Alaska, followed far behind by California, and then Oregon. Alaska is carrying the mantle greatly, as it has 141 of the 205 volcanoes in the country, which would make it the country with the 3rd most volcanoes in it on its own.

From looking at the choropleth on the left, we can see a clear pattern of volcanoes om the western states. This is because the west side of the country is on the Ring of Fire, making it more likely that we are to see this pattern of volcaneos appearing where they do.

The density graph on the bottom-left show the relationship between land area, and number of volcanoes. From the graph we can see that there is some correlation to these two attributes, such as USA, Russia, and Indonesia; they have a large land area, and a large number of volcanoes.

However, there are more exceptions than direct correlations. Antarctica, Canada, China, Australia. All have a large landmass, yet have no more than 35 volcanoes respectively. The key to how many volcanoes a country has, is directly related to where the country is. That is; if a country is where two tectonic plates meet eachother.

What type of volcanoes are around us, and what is their status?

Volcanos have a type and a status associated with them. Thiss heatmap plots the relationship between the type and status of volcanoes, and shows the cumulative number of volcanoes with a distinct type and status associated with it.

The type of a volcano denotes a volcaneos shape, size, and eruption style. Each type of volcano is created in a specific way, and some are more common than others.

For example, from this radial chart, we have the most common types of volcanoes going in a clockwise direction from the top. From this we can see that Stratovolcanoes are the most common type. This is because Stratovolcanoes form at convergent plate boundaries, where two plates are moving into eachother, and this is the most common type of plate boundary, therefore there are a subsequently large amount of these types of volcanoes.

The status of a volcano refers to when the last time the volcano was active. So a Historical status means it has been active in the last few thousand years, and a Radiocarbon status means it was active in the last 50,000 years.

The most common status of volcano is Holocene, with Historical volcanoes being very close in number. Holocene volcanoes have been active in the last 12,000 years, and Historical, the last few thousand. This is largely due to the fact that it is just easier to record volcanoes that are younger, as very old volcanoes that werent very large will have chnaged in shape, size, or even become extinct, and we may not be able to tell it was a volcano. Younger volcanoes have will be more likely to have the effects of their activity around for us to record.

How explosive are our volcanoes, and how often do they erupt?

Volcanic Explosivity Index or VEI, is a scale that ranges from 0-8 that measures the size of explosive volcanic eruptions. The VEI number is determined by considering the eruptions cloud height, the volume of material ejected (called the Tephra), and intensity of an eruption.

In the bubble timeline above, we can see the eruptions from the last 100 years, sorted by types of volcano. In this time we can see 2 main patterns: Stratovolcanoes have te most eruptions, which verifies the fact that most volcanoes are Stratovolcanoes, and that there were only 8 eruptions of VEI 5 or higher in the last 100 years.

The reason for this is down to two factors: Very large eruptions only occur over 50,000 year cycles, so we dont even see VEI 8 eruptions over this timeline. The second is that again, the older and smaller a volcano is, the harder it is to find evidence of its eruption.
